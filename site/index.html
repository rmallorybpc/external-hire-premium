S&P 500 CEO transitions, 2010 to 2022

What you pay for external talent does not predict what you get.

Boards pay a premium to hire an external CEO.

This study tests whether that premium buys a better result. Across 611 transitions, it does not.

Four sports free-agency studies reach the same answer.

611 transitions 158 external hires free public data 96 logged decisions
01

In plain terms

Companies often hire an external CEO. They usually pay more to do it. The bet is that an external hire beats a promotion from within.

This study tests that bet on 611 S&P 500 CEO changes. It measures what firms paid and what they got back.

What this research tracks

This study looks at Standard and Poor’s 500 (S&P 500) companies that changed their Chief Executive Officer (CEO) between 2010 and 2022. For each change, it first records one thing. Was the new CEO promoted from within (internal), or brought in from outside (external).

Then it measures four things.

  1. Pay. What the new CEO earns, against what the departing CEO earned. This is the premium. It is the cost of the hire.
  2. Stock performance. The return to shareholders, called Total Shareholder Return (TSR). It is the change in share price plus dividends, measured against the market. Above zero beat the market. Below zero lagged it.
  3. Operating performance. The profit a company earns from what it owns, called Return on Assets (ROA). It is net income divided by total assets. It shows how the business runs, apart from the stock price.
  4. Tenure. How long the new CEO stays in the job.

The study also checks how the company was performing before the hire. That shows what kind of firm reaches for an external hire.

Two claims are worth separating. One holds up. One does not.

What holds

The premium does not predict the return.

The amount a board pays over the departing CEO does not signal the return that follows. This holds after controls for firm size, year, and industry. It holds when the return is measured only after the hire.

premium to return: p = 0.14 (stock), 0.45 (operating)
What does not hold

External hires cost more and deliver less.

The cost premium points up, but it is not precise. The worse-return result is mostly selection. External CEOs are more often hired after the stock has lagged the market, not after operating decline. Pre-hire return was −18 pts vs market for external hires, −2 pts for internal (p < 0.001). The gap mostly disappears once the return is measured after the hire.

full window −38 pts (p < 0.001) to post-hire −12 pts (p = 0.14)

The picture

If paying more bought more, the line would slope up. It does not. The fitted line is flat. The premium carries no signal about the return.

03

The models

Three models carry the result. Each uses robust (HC1) errors. Controls are firm size (log assets), appointment year, and two-digit SIC industry.

Model 1. The premium

Do external hires cost more?

log( paysucc / paypred ) = β·External + controls + ε
β = +0.088   p = 0.54   n = 236

The premium points up and matches Bidwell in size (about 9%). It is not significant on the mean. It is significant on the median test (p = 0.007). The pay data is heavy-tailed, so the median is the steadier read.

Model 2. The return, after the hire

Do external hires deliver worse?

TSRpost = β·External + controls + ε
β = −0.119   p = 0.14   n = 431

The full window that straddles the hire gave −0.385 (p < 0.001). Most of that was decline the firm carried in before the hire. Measured only after the hire, the gap is not significant. Operating performance (ROA change) keeps a small effect: −0.025, p = 0.009.

Model 3. The headline

Does the premium predict the return?

Return = β·log( paysucc / paypred ) + controls + ε
β ≈ 0   p = 0.14 (stock), 0.45 (operating)   n = 189

The slope is flat. Paying a larger premium does not buy a better return. This survives every control and the post-hire window. It is the finding the study rests on.

Also tested. Tenure

Do external hires last as long?

h(t) = h0(t) · exp( β·External + controls )
hazard ratio = 1.28   p = 0.20

External CEOs leave a little sooner. The difference is not significant. Tenure comes from the transition chain, censored at the 2022 window end.

Methods

Every input is public. The whole study reproduces with no paid database. The sample was frozen before any financial data was pulled. The classification could not be tuned to the result.

SEC DEF 14A → paySEC XBRL → ROA public prices → TSRpoint-in-time S&P 500 robust (HC1) errorsindustry fixed effects

Sample   611 transitions, 2010 to 2022
Split   453 internal, 158 external (25.9%)
Pay   proxy table, outlier-screened
Returns   −2/+3yr and post-hire windows
Tenure   from the transition chain
Code   scripts and data in the repo

Audit trail

Every ruling that could move the sample is dated, sourced, and logged.

The rules were set before any coding. Each decision is recorded with its date, source, and reasoning. That covers every reclassification, every excluded firm, every manual correction. The sample is frozen. Results are reported with their uncertainty, including where they fall short of significance.

D-001 to D-096   full decision log, dated
Pre-committed   rules set before analysis
Frozen   sample locked before enrichment
Nulls shown   non-findings reported, not buried
